Stokes' Theorem (3D)

The grand generalization: Green's Theorem works for any surface in 3D space, not just flat regions. This is the full Stokes' Theorem.

The Full Statement

Stokes' Theorem

For a smooth surface $S$ with boundary curve $\partial S$:

Left side: Circulation around the boundary curve
Right side: Flux of curl through the surface

This is exactly Green's Theorem, but now $S$ can be a curved, tilted surface‚Äîa hemisphere, a paraboloid, a soap film stretched across a wire frame!

What's New in 3D?

The Curl is Now a Vector

In 2D, curl was just a number (positive = CCW, negative = CW). In 3D, curl is a full vector pointing along the rotation axis:

The magnitude tells you how fast the rotation is; the direction tells you the axis.

The Surface Has a Normal

At each point on the surface $S$, there's a normal vector $\mathbf{n}$ pointing perpendicular to the surface. The integrand $(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}$ measures how much the curl vector aligns with this normal.

üí° Physical Meaning

$(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n}$ measures the component of rotation that's "threaded through" the surface‚Äîperpendicular rotation doesn't contribute!

The Right-Hand Rule

The boundary orientation must match the surface orientation:

üëã The Right-Hand Rule

Point your right thumb in the direction of the surface normal $\mathbf{n}$. Your fingers curl in the direction you should traverse the boundary!

This ensures the theorem works with consistent signs.

Key Insight: Surface Independence

Here's something remarkable: the surface integral only depends on the boundary!

Surface Independence

If two surfaces $S_1$ and $S_2$ share the same boundary curve $C$, then:

$\iint_{S_1} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{S_2} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}$

You can compute on whichever surface is easier!

In the demo, you can change the surface shape while keeping the boundary fixed, and see that the curl flux stays the same!

Relationship to Other Theorems

Theorem Boundary Interior
Fund. Thm. Calculus $f(b) - f(a)$ $\int_a^b f'(x)\,dx$
Green's (2D) $\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ $\iint_R \text{curl}(\mathbf{F})\,dA$
Stokes' (3D) $\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ $\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}$
Gauss/Divergence $\oiint_{\partial V} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$ $\iiint_V \text{div}(\mathbf{F})\,dV$

All these theorems have the same structure: boundary integral = interior integral of a derivative.

Applications

  • Electromagnetism: Faraday's law of induction is Stokes' theorem applied to the electric field!
  • Fluid dynamics: Kelvin's circulation theorem uses Stokes' theorem
  • Differential geometry: Stokes' theorem generalizes to manifolds
  • Physics: Many conservation laws are expressed via these theorems
